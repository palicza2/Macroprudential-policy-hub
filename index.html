Global Executive Summary

  • Broad CCyB Tightening Amidst Divergent Risks: The European macroprudential landscape is characterized by a significant trend towards tightening the Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB), with the number of active countries projected to reach 25 by late 2025. This tightening is driven by concerns over robust credit dynamics and elevated residential real estate prices, as observed in countries like France and Cyprus. While some jurisdictions maintain a 0% CCyB rate due to subdued credit growth, the overall sentiment points towards building resilience against cyclical systemic risks, with nations like Bulgaria and Denmark implementing higher rates (2.0% and 2.5% respectively).
  • Strategic Recalibration of Systemic Risk Buffers: Systemic Risk Buffers (SyRB) are undergoing a strategic recalibration, shifting from general to more targeted, sector-specific interventions. While general SyRB usage is projected to decline to 1 by late 2025, sectoral SyRB adoption is accelerating, expected to reach 11 entities by the same period. This indicates a move towards precise risk management, with specific measures addressing risks within Commercial and Residential Real Estate sectors in countries like Italy, Lithuania, and Germany.
  • Jurisdictional Heterogeneity and Nuanced Calibration: A notable divergence exists in CCyB rates across Europe, ranging from 0% to 2.5%, reflecting varied perceptions of cyclical systemic risks and national policy priorities. Countries like Iceland, Denmark, Norway, and Bulgaria are at the higher end, while Austria, Finland, Italy, Malta, and the Netherlands maintain a 0% rate. This heterogeneity suggests that while some nations proactively increase buffers, others focus on other macroprudential tools or assess risks based on a broader set of indicators beyond standard credit-to-GDP gaps, as seen with Cyprus and Ireland.
  • Proactive and Neutral Stances in CCyB Implementation: Recent CCyB decisions up to January 2026 highlight a mixed landscape of tightening and neutral stances. Belgium and Poland are increasing their CCyB rates to 1.25% and 2.0% respectively, while Croatia and Cyprus are tightening to 1.5%. Conversely, Greece is adopting a neutral rate of 0.5% to build medium-term space, and Spain and Portugal are setting neutral buffers at 1.0% and 0.75% respectively. This demonstrates a divergence in approaches, with some countries opting for proactive tightening to address identified risks and others establishing neutral buffers to prepare for potential future stress.
