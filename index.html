Global Executive Summary

  • Overall Cycle: Cautious Tightening and Proactive Neutrality. The EU macroprudential landscape is characterized by a predominantly cautious approach, with many countries increasing or maintaining Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) rates. While traditional credit-to-GDP gaps often signal negative deviations (e.g., Austria's -2.37%), a growing number of authorities are adopting a "positive neutral rate" framework (e.g., Hungary's 1.0% from July 2025) to build resilience against persistent uncertainty and unforeseen shocks, driven by concerns over robust credit growth (e.g., Bulgaria's 89.1% credit-to-GDP in 2022).
  • CCyB Heterogeneity Reflects Divergent Risk Assessments. Significant divergence exists in CCyB rates across Europe, stemming from varying assessments of cyclical systemic risks and national economic conditions. While countries like Denmark, Iceland, and Norway maintain high buffers (2.5%) due to concerns over credit growth and asset prices, others like Austria, Finland, and the Netherlands hold a 0% rate due to subdued credit growth. This heterogeneity is further evidenced by recent increases in Belgium (1.25%) and Croatia (1.5%) driven by specific risks like mortgage loan portfolios and real estate growth, highlighting a tailored national approach beyond simple credit gap indicators.
  • Rise of Sectoral SyRB Targeting Real Estate Vulnerabilities. The Systemic Risk Buffer (SyRB) framework has strategically shifted towards a more targeted sectoral approach, with a pronounced focus on real estate exposures. While the general SyRB has stabilized, sectoral buffers, particularly for Residential and Commercial Real Estate (RRE/CRE), have seen substantial growth (e.g., Denmark's 7% for real estate companies, Austria's 1% for CRE). This recalibration, exemplified by Portugal's 4% SyRB on RRE-secured exposures and Italy's 1% on property-backed risks, indicates a proactive stance in mitigating systemic threats originating from these specific market segments.
  • Emerging Risks: Real Estate and Decoupling of Indicators. Macroprudential policy is increasingly grappling with emerging risks, notably within the real estate sector, and a growing decoupling between traditional indicators and policy decisions. For instance, Belgium's -34.4% credit gap is paired with a 1.25% CCyB, and Cyprus's -71.0% credit gap with a 1.5% CCyB, driven by concerns over credit expansion and property prices. This suggests that while the credit gap remains a foundational indicator, authorities are relying on a broader set of quantitative and qualitative assessments, including real estate market developments and private sector indebtedness, to calibrate buffers effectively in dynamic economic environments.

Part I: Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB)

Section Overview:
The current Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) landscape reflects a cautious approach to macroprudential policy, with a notable trend towards increasing or maintaining buffer rates across many European countries. While the traditional credit-to-GDP gap indicator often signals a negative deviation, suggesting a lack of cyclical risk (e.g., Austria's -2.37% credit gap in 2020, Belgium's -34.4% in 2025), a growing number of authorities are adopting a "positive neutral rate" framework. This approach, exemplified by Hungary's decision to set a 1.0% rate from July 2025, aims to build resilience even in the absence of overt overheating, acknowledging persistent macroeconomic uncertainty and the potential for unforeseen shocks. Drivers for increases often stem from concerns about robust credit growth, as seen in Bulgaria (89.1% credit-to-GDP in 2022), Croatia (15.4% y-o-y corporate loan growth in March 2023), and Cyprus (continued credit expansion to households and NFCs). Conversely, some countries maintain zero rates due to subdued credit growth or economic uncertainty, such as Finland and the Netherlands. The overall picture is one of a proactive stance, prioritizing financial sector resilience through buffer build-up, often guided by a forward-looking assessment that extends beyond simple cyclical indicators.

Part II: Systemic Risk Buffer (SyRB)

Section Overview:
The Systemic Risk Buffer (SyRB) has undergone a significant evolution, transitioning from a predominantly general buffer to a more targeted sectoral approach, reflecting a strategic response to emerging risks. Initially, the general buffer saw a steady increase from 1.0 in May 2014 to a peak of 14.0 by January 2019. However, the data reveals a clear shift towards sectoral buffers, particularly targeting the real estate sector, which has seen its allocation rise from 0.0 in early 2014 to 17.0 by July 2025. This strategic recalibration indicates a heightened focus on specific vulnerabilities within the financial system. The general buffer has remained relatively stable at 8.0 since January 2021, while the sectoral buffer, primarily driven by real estate exposures, has experienced substantial growth, reaching 14.0 by December 2024 and projected to hit 17.0 by mid-2025. This divergence underscores a proactive stance in addressing potential systemic threats originating from the real estate market.

Sectoral Focus

Sectoral Insight

The sectoral focus of Systemic Risk Buffers (SyRB) primarily targets risks associated with Residential Real Estate (RRE) and Commercial Real Estate (CRE) exposures. Several countries have implemented measures specifically for RRE, such as Portugal's 4% SyRB on IRB retail exposures secured by residential immovable property (PT.SRYB.1439), Italy's 1% SyRB on domestic credit and counterparty credit risks including retail exposures secured by residential property (IT.SRYB.1531), and Slovenia's reduction of its sectoral SyRB for retail exposures secured by residential immovable property to 0.5% (SI.SRYB.1505). Denmark also has a significant 7% SyRB for exposures to real estate companies (DK.SRYB.1537). Furthermore, Hungary's institution-specific SyRB is calibrated based on the contribution to systemic risk from problem commercial real estate exposures (HU.SRYB.1535), and Austria's new 1% sectoral SyRB applies to CRE exposures located in Austria (AT.SRYB.1739). These targeted measures indicate a clear focus on mitigating risks within the real estate sectors, which are often identified as sources of systemic vulnerability.
