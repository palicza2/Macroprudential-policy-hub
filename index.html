Global Executive Summary

The European macroprudential landscape is characterized by a growing reliance on both cyclical and structural policy tools, indicating a proactive stance against systemic risks. The Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) is experiencing a significant upward trend, with projections suggesting more than double the number of countries implementing positive rates by 2026 compared to 2022. This acceleration is driven by concerns over cyclical systemic risks, robust credit growth, and rising real estate prices, prompting countries to increase their CCyB requirements. While rates vary across the continent, with Norway, Sweden, and Poland leading in implementation, the overall direction points towards a more cautious approach to credit expansion and capital accumulation. The Credit Gap remains a pivotal indicator, with widening positive gaps often triggering higher CCyB rates to temper excessive lending and strengthen bank resilience.

Structural tools, particularly the Systemic Risk Buffer (SyRB), are being increasingly deployed, with a marked shift towards targeted sectoral interventions. The focus of these SyRB measures is prominently on real estate exposures, encompassing both commercial and residential real estate. Countries with significant real estate market exposures, such as Denmark and Norway, are utilizing these buffers to mitigate associated risks. Recent policy adjustments, including implementations and revisions of sectoral buffers for real estate in Germany and Slovenia, underscore the dynamic nature of SyRB application in response to market stabilization and evolving risk assessments. This targeted approach allows for a more precise calibration of macroprudential policy to specific vulnerabilities within the financial system.

The interaction between CCyB and SyRB highlights a multi-faceted strategy to manage systemic risks, with CCyB addressing cyclical overheating and SyRB targeting specific structural vulnerabilities. The increasing adoption of positive CCyB rates signifies a collective effort to build capital buffers during periods of credit expansion and asset price inflation, thereby enhancing the resilience of the banking sector to cyclical downturns. Simultaneously, the growing emphasis on sectoral SyRBs, especially for real estate, demonstrates a recognition that certain sectors may harbor inherent structural risks that require dedicated policy interventions. This dual approach allows policymakers to simultaneously manage the ebb and flow of credit cycles and address persistent vulnerabilities within specific market segments.

Overall, the macroprudential stance in Europe is becoming increasingly assertive and nuanced, reflecting a commitment to financial stability in the face of evolving economic conditions. The projected doubling of countries with positive CCyB rates by 2026, coupled with the targeted use of SyRBs for real estate, indicates a robust and adaptive policy framework. While some countries maintain zero rates, the prevailing trend is towards higher capital requirements and sector-specific interventions to preemptively address potential threats. This comprehensive approach, leveraging both cyclical and structural tools, aims to foster a more resilient and stable European financial system capable of withstanding future shocks.

The adoption of positive Countercyclical Capital Buffers (CCyB) has seen a significant upward trend, particularly accelerating from 2022 onwards. Initially, the number of countries implementing positive CCyB rates remained low and relatively stable between 2016 and 2019, hovering around 2-5 countries. A notable increase occurred in 2020 and 2021, pushing the count to around 10-12 countries, likely influenced by the economic uncertainties of the COVID-19 pandemic. However, the most dramatic growth phase began in 2022, with the number of countries adopting positive CCyB rates surging from approximately 12 to over 25 by 2026. This sharp increase suggests a widespread recognition of the need to build resilience in the banking sector against cyclical systemic risks, especially in the face of evolving geopolitical and macroeconomic uncertainties.
Historical Rates
The historical trajectories of Countercyclical Capital Buffers (CCyBs) reveal a general trend of increasing rates from the mid-2010s, with many countries implementing or raising their CCyB rates, particularly in the late 2010s and early 2020s, often in response to perceived cyclical systemic risks and robust credit growth, as seen in Germany's increase to 0.75% in early 2022 and Denmark's significant rise to 2.5% in early 2022. Conversely, some countries like Malta and Liechtenstein maintained a 0% CCyB rate throughout the observed period, citing negative credit gaps and a lack of excessive credit growth, while others, such as the Czech Republic, have recently decided to decrease their CCyB rate, reflecting a shift in risk assessment. The COVID-19 pandemic appears to have influenced some decisions, with authorities often opting to maintain or lower rates initially due to heightened uncertainty, though many have since begun to increase them again as economic conditions stabilized and new risks emerged. Several countries, including Latvia, Hungary, and Poland, are adopting or planning to adopt a "positive neutral" CCyB framework, aiming to build buffers even in periods of standard risk to enhance resilience against unforeseen shocks.

Detailed Analysis

The current Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) rates exhibit a wide range across European countries, with Norway, Sweden, and Poland leading with the highest rates at 2.5% and 2% respectively, indicating a proactive stance against potential financial imbalances. Conversely, several nations including Liechtenstein, Malta, Austria, Finland, and Germany maintain a CCyB rate of 0%, reflecting a more cautious approach or a lack of identified cyclical risks. Other countries like Ireland, Slovakia, and Belgium are implementing or have recently increased their CCyB rates to levels between 1.25% and 1.5%, signaling a moderate build-up of buffers in response to evolving economic conditions. This varied landscape of CCyB rates underscores the diverse economic outlooks and risk assessments across the region, with authorities adjusting their macroprudential policies to safeguard financial stability.
Risk Analysis
The Credit Gap and the Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) are distinct but related macroprudential tools used to manage systemic risk in the financial sector. The Credit Gap, as depicted in the scatter plot, measures the deviation of the credit-to-GDP ratio from its long-term trend, indicating periods of excessive credit growth (positive gap) or subdued credit growth (negative gap). A substantial positive credit gap often signals overheating in the credit market and potential financial imbalances, which is a key trigger for activating or increasing the CCyB. The CCyB, represented by the 'rate' on the y-axis, is a capital requirement imposed on banks that can be adjusted based on the assessment of cyclical systemic risks, primarily informed by the credit gap and other indicators. For instance, countries like Germany and Denmark have increased their CCyB rates to 0.75% and 2.5% respectively, coinciding with positive or widening credit gaps, aiming to build resilience against potential downturns. Conversely, countries like Malta and Luxembourg, with significantly negative credit gaps, have maintained or set their CCyB rates at zero or low levels, indicating a lack of excessive credit growth and thus no immediate need for a CCyB activation. The relationship is thus one where a widening credit gap often leads to a higher CCyB rate, acting as a countercyclical measure to curb excessive credit and bolster bank capital.

Systemic Risk Buffer (SyRB)

Section Summary
    Here's a high-level summary of the SyRB section:
  • Systemic Risk Buffer (SyRB) measures are increasingly utilized, with a notable shift towards sectoral interventions, particularly targeting real estate exposures (CRE & RRE). Countries like Denmark, Norway, and Portugal exhibit significant exposures, with Denmark's being heavily influenced by real estate.
  • Recent decisions highlight a dynamic application of SyRBs, with countries like Germany and Slovenia implementing or adjusting sectoral buffers for real estate to address market stabilization and cyclical risks. Conversely, some countries are reducing or de-activating existing buffers, indicating a responsive approach to evolving systemic risk assessments.
Adoption Trend
The usage of SyRB measures has shown a significant increase in general measures from 2015 to 2020, peaking at 12 measures before a subsequent decline. Sectoral measures, on the other hand, remained low and stable at 1 measure from 2015 to early 2021. Following this period, both general and sectoral measures began a gradual upward trend, with sectoral measures showing a more pronounced increase from 2022 onwards. By 2026, both general and sectoral measures are projected to reach approximately 11 and 8 measures respectively. This indicates a shift towards more active use of SyRB measures, with a notable rise in sectoral interventions in recent years.
Sectoral Focus
The sectoral composition of SyRB exposures varies significantly across countries, with Denmark exhibiting the highest overall exposure primarily driven by Real Estate (CRE & RRE). Following Denmark, Norway shows a substantial exposure dominated by General sector, while Portugal's exposure is largely attributed to Residential Real Estate (RRE). Several countries, including Belgium, Iceland, and Sweden, display a consistent exposure rate of 3% across multiple sectors, predominantly General and Real Estate (CRE & RRE). In contrast, countries like Lithuania, Germany, and Hungary show lower exposures, with Hungary having a notable Commercial Real Estate (CRE) component. The remaining countries generally have lower exposures, with a mix of General, Real Estate (CRE & RRE), and Other sectors represented.

Active Systemic Risk Buffer (SyRB) measures demonstrate a dynamic and targeted approach to financial stability, with a notable concentration on real estate exposures and a general trend towards recalibration. While general SyRBs are applied country-wide in nations like Finland and Croatia, many countries, including Austria, Italy, Germany, and Portugal, are implementing or adjusting sectoral buffers specifically for commercial and residential real estate. These sectoral measures often aim to mitigate risks associated with lending growth, property collateral, and cyclical economic conditions, as seen in Denmark and Lithuania. Furthermore, the data reveals a pattern of buffer resets and reductions, such as in Sweden and Iceland, suggesting an ongoing assessment of systemic risk levels and a willingness to adapt buffer requirements accordingly. Some countries, like the Czech Republic, also consider broader economic factors such as openness and energy transformation risks in their general SyRB frameworks. The variety in application, from broad country-wide buffers to institution-specific or sector-specific ones, highlights the tailored nature of these regulatory tools.
